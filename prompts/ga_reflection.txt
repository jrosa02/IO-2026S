You are tuning hyperparameters for a Genetic Algorithm (GA) agent that solves a combinatorial scheduling problem.

## The problem
A single machine must process n jobs. Every job i has a processing time p_i, an earliness
penalty weight a_i, and a tardiness penalty weight b_i. All jobs share one common due date:
  d = h * sum(p_i),  where h in (0,1) controls tightness
  (h close to 0 → very tight schedule, most jobs late; h close to 1 → loose, most jobs early)

The agent searches for a job ordering (permutation) that minimises total weighted cost:
  cost = sum_i [ a_i * max(0, d - C_i) + b_i * max(0, C_i - d) ]
where C_i is the completion time of job i in the chosen order.

There is no formula that gives the optimal order directly — the agent must search.

## How the Genetic Algorithm works here
The GA maintains a population of candidate job orderings (permutations). Each generation:
1. Elite individuals (top elite_size by cost) are copied unchanged to the next generation.
2. The remaining slots are filled by Order Crossover (OX) of two selected parents.
3. Each child is mutated with probability mutation_rate (one random swap applied).
4. Every reoptimize_every steps during the episode, the GA re-runs for `generations`
   generations starting from the current best solution.

The GA runs as an inner loop inside the episode: it is re-triggered periodically, so
the total number of GA generations executed = (n_steps / reoptimize_every) * generations.

## Hyperparameter effects
- population_size: more individuals → broader search per generation, but each generation
  costs more time (fewer total calls fit in the step budget). Sweet spot typically 20–100.
- generations: GA iterations per re-optimisation call. More generations → deeper search
  each time it fires. Increasing this while reducing reoptimize_every can trade breadth
  for depth.
- mutation_rate: probability of applying one random swap to a child after crossover.
  Too low → population converges to a clone of the best (premature convergence).
  Too high → search becomes random; children look nothing like their parents.
  Useful range: 0.01–0.3.
- elite_size: top individuals carried over unchanged. Prevents losing the best solution
  found so far. Should be small relative to population_size (1–5 typically).
- reoptimize_every: how often (in episode steps) the GA is re-invoked. Smaller values
  → more frequent GA calls → more generations total, but each call starts from less
  episode context. Larger values → fewer calls but each starts from a more refined base.

## How to interpret execution results
- improvement_pct: percentage reduction from initial greedy cost to best found.
  < 5%  → very poor; population may have converged too fast or mutation is too low.
  5-20% → weak; try larger population or higher mutation_rate.
  20-40% → solid.
  > 40% → excellent; fine-tune mutation or elite_size for marginal gains.

- convergence pattern:
  "converged very early then plateaued" → population converged prematurely; raise
    mutation_rate or increase population_size to maintain diversity.
  "steadily improving with no clear plateau" → step budget too short or reoptimize_every
    too large; decrease reoptimize_every so the GA fires more often.
  "gradual improvement with plateauing toward end" → healthy behaviour.

- n_improvements / n_steps: low ratio means the GA keeps re-discovering solutions it
  already knows; increase population_size or mutation_rate to introduce new candidates.

- zero beneficial moves → mutation_rate too low or elite_size too large relative to
  population_size; the population is homogeneous.
